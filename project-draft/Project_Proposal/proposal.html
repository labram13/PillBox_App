INFO 340 Project Proposal

Problem/Data Domain

1. Overview

As a group, we decided we wanted to tackle the issue of prescription medication adherence. The World Health Organization defines adherence as "the degree to which [a patient's] behavior corresponds with the agreed recommendations from a health care provider." When a patient forgets which meds they take, doesn't take the appropriate number of doses each week, and forgets to refill their prescriptions on time, they are exhibiting poor adherence, and there is a deterioration in their health outcomes. It also makes it harder for their healthcare provider to help them, since the patient isn't doing their part to stay on top of their health.

2. Information technology problem

Pronouncing and recalling long lists of medication names is no easy task. Many people struggle with communicating every medication they are on with their providers for those reasons. Further, keeping track of how many pills are left in a prescription medication bottle can be difficult. Doing so meticulously requires the physical dexterity and acuity of vision to handle little pills, as well as the intellectual capability of counting up to large numbers. Additionally, not all pill bottles are transparent, adding to the difficulty. Finally, resetting a pillbox each week can be challenging, because a patient must rely on their memory of the past week to reset the pillbox. Human memory is fallible, thus an information problem presents itself in that capacity.

3. What has already been tried

Physical pillboxes are an elegant solution to the issue of medication adherence. However, we hope to build on top of the limits of pillboxes to make adherence an even easier task for patients. The information problems in this area which we mention above are not currently adequately addressed by physical pillboxes. Duke PillBox is a preexisting attempt to address the issue of adherence. However, the app simply tells a patient's provider how good they are at keeping up with their doses. It does not actually empower the patient to do a better job of keeping track of their meds. At the end of the day, physicians can't take pills on their patient's behalf. Therefore Duke PillBox is not a robust solution to the issue.

App Description

1. Who are the users?

Patients with at least one medication prescription will compose our user base. A vast majority of our users will include individuals with multiple prescriptions. This app will ideally help those with complex and acute medical needs, as they are the folks who tend to be on a higher number of medications. When a large number of medications are prescribed it can be hard for the patients to keep track of when to use them, how frequently they need to take the drugs, and common symptoms that can come up. Furthermore, individuals with physical disabilities and the elderly stand to benefit greatly from the app, as they may not possess the fine motor skills needed to easily handle and count pills. Those with intellectual disabilities and, again, the elderly, are likely to be supported by our app, which handles dose counting and information storage on their behalf. Our application will allow those users to more easily stick with an adherence plan and feel like an empowered agent in the improvement of their own health.

2. What will the users do?

Users will be able to interact with three main features:

  1. First, they will be able to search a list of the 300 most common medications, then save the meds they take to a personal list which users can show their healthcare providers when they are physically at a checkup.
  2. Then, they will be able to sort their selected medications into a "weekly pill planner". The weekly pill planner will mirror what a patient's physical pill organizer should look like at the beginning of each week before the medications have been depleted.
  3. Finally, patients will be able to input the number of pills they have, as well as the number of pills they take each week, into a "prescription countdown" feature. At the end of each week, patients must check a box saying they took all of their pills that week. Checking the box will deduct the number of pills per week from the total number of pills. When the total number of pills is close to being completely depleted, users will be notified that it's time to contact their pharmacy or healthcare provider for a refill.

3. What kind of information will the users be viewing?

Once again, users will be able to view and interact with this list of the 300 most common medications. To be more specific, users are able to view the details of these medications, refill information, and medication reminders. Every feature our users interface with will be derived from this information source.

3. What kind of information will the users be viewing?

Once again, users will be able to view and interact with this list of the 300 most common medications. Every feature our users interface with will be derived from this information source.

4. How will the app help?

Searching and storing information from a list of canonical medication spellings will address the issue of medication names being difficult to recall and pronounce. Our "weekly pill planner" will remove the onus of remembering how to reset a physical pillbox each week. And counting pills, as well as remembering to refill prescriptions will be handled by our "prescription countdown" feature. With regards to the connection between these information issues and adherence: the list search will make it easier for patients and providers to agree on adherence expectations by facilitating accurate and informed communication, and the weekly planner will ensure that patients are continuously aware of their required dose, and the countdown will make patients less likely to skip doses while waiting for a refill.
